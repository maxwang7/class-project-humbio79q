Identity

As humans, we have a certain biological sex: male, female, or intersex. But that biological sex doesn’t necessarily reflect our sexual gender identity, the gender we feel, or our sexual orientation, the gender of the people we are attracted to.

Your identity isn’t a word. It’s who you are. And even though some of these words may help you describe who you are, don’t use it as a label for yourself. You goal is not to find a word, but to find yourself!

There are a lot of ways that people identify. Here’s a short list we’ve put together. It’s not comprehensive, but it may start helping you think about your own identity:

Gay
someone that's attracted (emotionally, sexually, romantically) to someone of the same gender
Lesbian
a gay woman
Bisexual
someone that's attracted to more than one sex, gender, or gender identity
Transgender
describes people whose gender identity is different from the gender they were assigned at birth. Being transgender does not imply anything about your sexual orientation.
Asex
people who don't feel sexual attraction for others, but may still feel an emotional attraction.
Ally
a person who isn't LGBT, but shows support for LGBT people
Closeted
used to describe an LGBT person who has not disclosed their sexual orientation or gender identity

Healthy Relationships

There are a huge variety of relationships. But there’s also a lot of commonality. In most health relationships, both people have an emotional attachment that keeps them together long term, and they usually feel good about being together. And not just feel good about each other, but feel good about themselves. There are a lot of things that you can do to make a healthy relationship:

Trust
Trust is key. Knowing that your partner will be there for you and will be honest gives you confidence and builds long term commitment.
Communication and Openness
Sharing is caring. If something bothers you, say it! If something great happened to you today, say it! If you pet the cutest puppy, say it! Healthy relationships are built on these shared memories and experiences, but you can’t build it if you don’t share them.
A willingness to work at it together
Even in a healthy relationship, a lot of things aren’t going to go right. There will be miscommunications, tough situations, and fights. But being able to stick it through -- that can make all the difference.

Healthy Sexuality

Your sexuality is an important part of your identity. Sexuality is composed of a lot of things: your sexual orientation, your sex identification, and your biological sex. The important thing is to think about how your feel and to not have those feelings be lost by what others tell you. Let’s think about each of these parts.

Biological sex is the sex you’re born with, male, female, or intersex. What’s yours?

Sex identification is how you choose to express your sexuality. It’s not connected with your biological sex. Transsexuals are people who identify as a different sex than their biological sex. Many have stories of growing up being treated as one sex but feeling that they’re the other. There are a lot of those stories online, some of them can be found here. How do you identify?

Sexual orientation is who you’re attracted to. Straight describes those that are attracted to people of the opposite gender. Gay describes those that are attracted to people of the same gender. Gay is often used to describe males who are attracted to males, and lesbian is used to describe females that females. Many that are gay go through a “coming out” process. Again, there are a lot of these stories online (try here), and they may help you understand your feelings. Who are you attracted to?

Exploring Messages: School

You get a lot of messages from school about your sexuality and relationships. It isn’t just sex ed. You get messages from your friends, your teachers, and the groups you participate in. They show you the “right” way to ask someone on a date. They call some people “needy” relationships and and others “distant.” It’s important to think about how these messages may influence your thinking. List some that you think of.

Family

You probably think talking to your parents about sex is awkward. The thing is, they are probably just as scared to bring up the topic with you. Can you imagine how your parents felt when they had the first “sex talk” with you? They probably rehearsed the entire thing a dozen times.

But talking about your sexuality with your parents is important. They can be a source of support and tell you about their experience growing up. It may seem awkward, but it can be a great experience for you and them.
